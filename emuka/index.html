Economy Market Watch

By Dawit Tolosa

Rising Goods Prices Keep Squeezing Families in Hawassa

Residents say daily essentials remain painfully expensive even as national inflation cools.

Residents of Hawassa say the trip to the market has become a routine of difficult trade-offs as the cost of basic goods stays stubbornly high.

For Genet, a mother in the city, cooking oil captures the pressure clearly. A five-liter bottle that once sold for 1,300 to 1,600 birr can now reach about 2,500 birr, forcing her family to ration every drop.

Local traders say they are not raising prices lightly. They point to fuel costs, foreign-currency shortages, and supply chain disruption that make it slower and more expensive to restock shop shelves.

Hawassa University economist Henok Sisay said the pressure is coming from both global and domestic shocks. He cited the Russia-Ukraine war's effect on imported fuel and commodities, along with the economic damage caused by conflict in northern Ethiopia.

He noted that national inflation has fallen from around 36 percent to about 9 percent under tighter monetary policy, but households say that relief has not yet reached shop counters in any meaningful way.

For now, families across Hawassa say they are still buying less, switching brands, and delaying other needs just to keep food on the table.

Health Wellness Report

By Dagmawit Dera

Traditional and Modern Therapy Blend Near Hawassa

Practitioners near Wendo Genet say sand-walking therapy is gaining attention as communities revisit affordable, culturally-rooted care.

Traditional medicine is receiving renewed attention in and around Hawassa as practitioners and health professionals revisit reflexology-like treatments alongside modern care.

One therapy session described by local doctor Yodahe is taking place in Wendo Genet, where patients walk barefoot on warm sand as part of treatment.

He said the practice is believed to stimulate pressure points in the feet, improve circulation, and reduce stress. Health workers are now trying to understand the method more clearly through modern medical study.

Supporters say such approaches remain affordable and culturally accepted for many families, especially in places where formal care can feel distant or expensive.

Health experts also caution that wider use should be backed by research, standardization, and clinical evaluation so traditional therapies can be integrated safely into formal healthcare systems.

Transport Tariff Watch

By Nejat Mehamed

Hawassa Faces a Rising Tariff Burden as Commuters and Drivers Push Back

New transport rates, fuel shortages, and higher spare-part costs are intensifying pressure on both operators and passengers.

A wave of frustration is sweeping through Hawassa as drivers, students, and merchants grapple with higher transportation costs on top of an already difficult cost-of-living climate.

Driver Arega, who operates routes from Hawassa to hubs such as Addis Ababa and Adama, said a 10 birr increase has been added across routes while fuel shortages remain severe enough to keep drivers waiting in line for days.

Short-distance operators and commuters say the strain runs in both directions. Some drivers argue the revised tariff still does not reflect the true price of fuel, while residents like Dawit say rising food costs are already pushing people to walk instead of paying for transport.

Shopkeeper Ali said the tariff changes spread through the wider economy because merchants eventually pass extra transportation costs into the price of food, clothing, and other daily goods.

Public transportation specialist Ato Degeife said the increase was issued under a federal mandate and based on research that considered vehicle type, age, road conditions, and rising operating costs. He said petrol has climbed from 91 birr per liter last year to 129 birr.

Officials say inspections are being carried out three times a month to stop overcharging, and tariff updates are being shared through social media and radio. Even so, public anger remains high.

Tourism Urban Design

By Tigist Anteneh

Hawassa City Plans a New Lakeside Development Around Lake Hawassa

Officials say the phased project is meant to protect the lake, expand recreation, and make the waterfront more welcoming for residents and visitors.

Hawassa City Administration says it has launched a major lakeside development project around Lake Hawassa as part of the city's broader urban development strategy.

Elias Welde, Design and Contract Administration Team Leader at the Hawassa City Construction Department, said the project is meant to improve public use of the area while protecting the lake environment.

The plan includes recreational areas, walking paths, and improved public facilities designed to make the lakeside more attractive and accessible for both residents and visitors.

City leaders also believe the development could strengthen the local economy by attracting more tourists to Hawassa, which is already known for its natural setting.

Residents have responded positively. Yayenehtesfaye Mengistu said the project could create better places for families and visitors to spend time, while also urging officials to keep services affordable for the wider community.

Authorities say implementation will happen in phases and is expected to contribute to Hawassa's long-term development.

Sports Youth Football

By Meti Gemechu

From Dust to Dreams: Hawassa's Young Footballers Rise at Korem Meda

The historic field keeps producing ambition even as coaches and families call for better sports facilities across the city.

As the sun dips below the horizon, clouds of dust rise over Korem Meda, where hundreds of young athletes gather each day hoping to build football careers from a shared community ground.

The field is known for producing Ethiopian players such as Adane Girma and Shimelis Bekele, and it still stands as a symbol of hope for many children in Hawassa.

City officials say that enthusiasm for sport is doing more than filling fields. They see it as part of a wider push toward health, community development, and stronger neighborhood life.

According to the Hawassa City Administration Sports Commission, the city has between 15 and 20 sports fields. Many fall below standard, but they remain heavily used for training and exercise.

Commissioner Damarso Dona said recent urban projects, including cycling paths and recreational spaces, have increased participation. The city has also introduced street races and organized cycling events.

Coaches say space is still a major challenge. Sintayehu Meshesha, who runs a youth football project, said his team trains on only half of the Korem Meda field three times a week.

Authorities say new standards introduced under a sports policy should help improve facilities in the coming years, but for now young players are still chasing big dreams from dusty ground.

Community Humanitarian Work

Rohobot Founder Addise Mekebo Turned a Dream Into a Lifeline for Vulnerable Children

After leaving a 23-year career in education, Addise Mekebo says faith, sacrifice, and community support helped build a home for abandoned infants.

Addise Mekebo's story began in Kembata, where he worked as a teacher and school principal for 23 years before a dream pushed him toward a very different life.

In that dream, he saw distressed children wandering the streets and heard a message telling him to care for them like a father. He later left his stable job and founded Rohobot, a humanitarian organization whose name reflects the idea that God makes room for people to flourish.

The early years were extremely difficult. Mekebo says he struggled to secure legal permits, lost the security of a monthly salary, and at one point had so little money that he went through the Easter holiday without food until a friend helped him.

The COVID-19 period brought the deepest crisis. Donations stopped, board members left, and he was left caring for 25 infants with no clear way to feed them.

Mekebo says he responded by returning to Durame and selling inherited land to buy milk and diapers. After that sacrifice, support started to grow, including a 17-month rent waiver from his landlady and wider public attention.

Today Rohobot works with police in Sidama and nearby areas to rescue abandoned infants. The organization says 310 children have since been legally adopted into new families.

Mekebo's message remains simple: people should stop living only for themselves and share even a small part of the world's problems.
