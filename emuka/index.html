Economy Cost Of Living

Rising Price of Goods in Hawassa City

Families say basic items are consuming a larger share of their income, even as inflation slows on paper.

Residents of Hawassa are facing increasing financial pressure as the price of basic goods continues to rise, making it difficult for many families to afford essential items.

Ms. Genet, a city resident, explained that the persistent increase in prices has made it increasingly challenging to meet daily needs. She said that a 5-liter bottle of cooking oil previously sold for between 1,300 and 1,600 Birr, but now costs as much as 2,050 to 2,500 Birr. This sharp rise has forced her to reduce consumption and carefully ration her supplies. Her experience reflects a wider trend among consumers who say their purchasing power has declined noticeably.

Traders attribute the surge in prices to rising fuel costs and ongoing supply chain disruptions. According to them, higher transportation expenses have significantly contributed to increased market prices for goods.

According to Mr. Henok Sisay, an economist and teacher at Hawassa University, both global and domestic pressures are driving inflation. Globally, the Russia-Ukraine conflict has destabilized supply chains and increased import costs. Domestically, the impact of recent conflict in northern Ethiopia has reduced local production, strained foreign currency reserves, and limited the supply of goods.

Mr. Henok additionally mentioned that inflation has shown signs of slowing, dropping from 36% to around 9%. He said the country's tight monetary policies have also helped stabilize the economy.

Even with inflation easing, many households in Hawassa continue to struggle with the cost of living. Without meaningful improvements in supply conditions and broader economic recovery, basic goods may remain out of reach for a growing number of residents.

Transport Tariff Debate

Hawassa Faces Rising Tariff Burden

Drivers, students, and small business owners say higher transport costs are worsening the pressure on daily life.

Hawassa drivers, students, and shopkeepers are becoming increasingly frustrated with the recent increase in transportation costs, raising concerns about the city's economic stability and the government's ability to balance revenue needs with what residents can realistically afford.

Residents say the rise on main highways has worsened the effects of fuel shortages and rising spare-part costs, making it difficult for households and businesses to cope. Arega, a driver on routes from Hawassa to Addis Ababa, Butajira, Hosanna, and Adama, said the tariff added 10 Birr on all routes and that fuel remains the main cause. He added that he had to stand in line for five days to refill.

Mebrate, who drives between Hawassa and Yirgalem, said the change feels out of step with reality because passengers often do not know the official rates and may protest. Dawit, a teacher balancing work and master's studies at Hawassa University, said the increase in tariffs does not take into consideration what consumers can truly afford.

Ali, a Hawassa shopkeeper, said the fuel problem affects both workers and traders. He explained that merchants are being pushed into raising the prices of food, clothing, and other essentials, even when they know customers are already under strain.

Hawassa region public transportation specialist Ato Degeife said the increase was mandated by the federal government and signed by the transport minister rather than being a municipal decision. He explained that the rates were based on vehicle type, age, and road condition, and said enforcement teams try to monitor the city's tariff practices three times a month.

He added that the bureau uses Facebook, Telegram, and radio airtime on Sidama Broadcasting Corporation to inform the public. Even so, the frustration on the ground shows how difficult it remains to balance transport economics with everyday living costs.

Education Digital Shift

Sidama Region Prepares for Online National Exams

Schools and institutions are expanding technical readiness as more students prepare to sit for university entrance exams online.

The 12th-grade university entrance exam for the 2018 E.C. in the Sidama Region is being prepared to be administered largely online, according to Examination Team Leader Mr. Tessema Tesfaye.

He said the team is currently identifying the number of computers, internet capacity, and generator power available in the region's higher education institutions and colleges. Out of the 25,123 students to be examined in the region this year, approximately 9,000 are expected to take the exam online, while the remaining students will enter their respective universities to take the exam in person.

Students taking the exam online will do so at Hawassa University campuses including Bensa Daye Campus, Yirgalem Campus, Agriculture Campus, Techo Campus, Main Campus, and Rift Valley College.

Mr. Tessema said shortages of computers and interruptions in electricity and network service remain major challenges. He added that officials are installing reliable generators and working with Ethio Telecom management to reduce network disruptions.

Students with disabilities or those requiring special support will be identified during registration, and support will be provided based on need. For students entering universities to take the exam, medical centers and ambulances are also being prepared for emergencies.

School leaders say they are reinforcing lessons, running tutorials, conducting online model exams, and inviting psychological experts to help students become mentally prepared. The new system is also seen as a significant step toward reducing cheating and promoting fairer results.
