Tourism Lakeside Project

Hawassa City Plans Lakeside Development Project to Improve Tourism and Public Spaces

City officials say the new lakeside project is designed to protect the environment while creating more welcoming spaces for residents and visitors.

The Hawassa City Administration has launched a new development project around Lake Hawassa aimed at improving tourism, protecting the environment, and creating better public spaces for residents.

According to Hawassa City Construction Department Design and Contract Administration Team Leader Mr. Elias Welde, the work forms part of the city's broader urban development plan and will focus on improving the lakeside area while maintaining environmental sustainability.

Officials say the project will help protect the lake environment while also creating modern recreational spaces for both residents and visitors.

Plans include recreational areas, walking paths, and other facilities meant to make the lakeside more attractive and accessible to the public. City leaders also expect the project to support the local economy by drawing more tourists to the area.

Residents have welcomed the initiative. One resident, Mr. Yayenehtesfaye Mengistu, said the development could make the lakeside more attractive and provide better places for families and visitors to spend time.

He also urged the city to keep recreational services affordable so that the wider community can benefit, not only tourists or higher-income residents.

Officials say the development will be implemented in phases and is expected to contribute to the long-term growth of Hawassa City.

Sports Youth Development

From Dust to Glory: Hawassa's Sports Infrastructure Inspires a New Generation

Around Korem Mada, young athletes keep training through dust and limited space, hoping local investment can match their ambition.

As the sun sets over Korem Mada, clouds of dust rise from the historic field where hundreds of young athletes gather daily, chasing dreams of becoming professional footballers.

The ground is known for producing notable Ethiopian players such as Adane Girma and Shimelis Bekele, and it remains a symbol of hope for many of Hawassa's children.

For families across the city, football is more than a pastime. It is a pathway to represent the country, build a better future, and take part in a wider culture of health and community development.

Hawassa City Administration Sports Commission officials say the city has between 15 and 20 sports fields. Many fall below standard, but they are still heavily used for exercise and training.

Commissioner Damarso Dona said new facilities and cycling paths introduced through urban development projects have encouraged more people to take part in physical activity. The city has also launched mass sports programs such as street races and organized cycling events.

Challenges remain. Coach Sintayehu Meshesha, who runs a youth football project, said limited space forces his team to train on only half of the Korem Mada field three times a week.

Parents share similar concerns and want stronger support for youth development in neighborhoods across the city. Officials acknowledge the poor condition of many fields but say new standards and a sports policy are expected to improve the sector in the coming years.

Public Safety Crime Watch

Mobile Phone Theft on the Rise Across Hawassa as Thieves Shift Tactics

Police and residents say phone theft is becoming more organized, with cases reported in dormitories, on the street, and from passing motorbikes.

Phone theft is becoming a growing concern in Hawassa City as thieves use different methods such as pickpocketing, breaking into university dormitories, and snatching phones from pedestrians using motorbikes.

According to Hawassa City Police Criminal Investigation Department Vice Inspector Yrdachew Angiso, the city records two to three phone theft cases every week.

He said many suspects are young people from rural areas or the streets who are influenced by peer pressure or drawn to crime by the hope of quick money. Some operate in groups and frequently change tactics, making them harder to catch.

Victims say many thefts happen in the evening or late at night when people are less alert. Phones are often taken while owners are sleeping, walking alone, or using public transportation.

University student Kena Deresa said her phone was stolen while she was asleep in her dormitory. Another student, Etsubdink Abraham, said men on a motorbike snatched her friend's phone while the student was returning to campus from work.

Hawassa resident Ethiopia Kebede said her phone disappeared while she was visiting a friend near Piassa, showing that theft is also common in busy public areas.

Police say they are working through the Community Policing Program and using both uniformed and plainclothes officers across the city. Residents are being advised to stay alert, especially in crowded or isolated areas, and to avoid openly displaying phones in public.
