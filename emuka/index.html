Economy Cost of Living

Rising Food Prices Deepen Cost-of-Living Pressure in Hawassa

Residents say the price of basic goods keeps climbing, leaving many families struggling to cover everyday household needs.

Residents of Hawassa say they are under growing financial pressure as the cost of basic goods continues to rise, making it harder for families to afford essential items.

One resident, Ms. Genet, said the increase is especially painful for low- and middle-income households that already budget carefully from month to month.

She pointed to cooking oil as one clear example. A five-liter bottle that once sold for about 1,300 to 1,600 birr now costs roughly 2,050 to 2,500 birr, forcing her family to cut consumption and ration supplies more strictly.

Traders say the surge is tied to higher fuel prices and ongoing supply disruptions. They argue that transport costs have risen sharply, and those extra expenses are being passed on to consumers in the market.

Economist and Hawassa University teacher Mr. Henok Sisay said both global and domestic pressures are feeding the inflation. He cited the Russia-Ukraine war, which disrupted international supply chains and raised import costs, along with the economic effects of conflict in northern Ethiopia, which reduced local production and strained foreign currency reserves.

He added that inflation has slowed compared with earlier highs, dropping from about 36 percent to around 9 percent, partly because of tighter monetary policies. Even so, households say the lower inflation rate has not yet brought real relief at the market level.

As prices remain elevated, many families in Hawassa say they are still being forced to buy less, switch to cheaper goods, and delay other household expenses just to keep food on the table.

Education Online Exams

Sidama Region Prepares for a Larger Shift to Online National Exams

Education officials say thousands of Grade 12 students are expected to sit this year's university entrance exam online, but equipment, electricity, and network reliability remain key concerns.

Sidama Region is preparing to administer a large share of the 2018 E.C. university entrance examination online, according to Examination Team Leader Mr. Tessema Tesfaye.

He said officials are currently checking the number of available computers, internet capacity, and generator power in higher education institutions and colleges across the region.

Out of the 25,123 students expected to take the exam this year, about 9,000 are projected to sit online, while the remaining candidates will take the exam at their assigned university locations.

Students scheduled for online testing are expected to use Hawassa University campuses including Bensa Daye, Yirgalem, the Agriculture Campus, Techo Campus, and Main Campus, as well as Rift Valley College.

Officials say the biggest challenges are shortages of computers and recurring interruptions in electricity and network service. To reduce those risks, schools and authorities are working on generator backup plans and coordinating with Ethio Telecom to improve connectivity.

Special support is also being planned for students with disabilities and others who need additional assistance during registration and on exam days. Medical centers and ambulances are being prepared for students who may need urgent care.

School leaders say preparation is already under way through tutorials, early syllabus completion, revision sessions, and online model exams. Students interviewed for the story said they are using the extra support to strengthen both their subject knowledge and computer confidence.

Educators add that the online system is also being seen as an important step in reducing cheating and building confidence that exam results better reflect student ability.

Transport Tariff Watch

New Transport Tariffs Stir Frustration Among Drivers and Passengers in Hawassa

Residents, drivers, and small business owners say the latest fare increase is making movement around the city even harder to afford.

The recent increase in transportation tariffs around Hawassa is drawing frustration from drivers, students, and shopkeepers who say it adds new pressure to an already difficult economy.

Residents say the higher fares are compounding the effects of fuel shortages and rising spare-part prices, making it more expensive to travel to work, school, and market centers.

Driver Arega, who works routes between Hawassa and cities such as Addis Ababa, Butajira, Hosanna, and Adama, said the tariff has increased by 10 birr on routes while fuel remains the biggest pressure on operators. He said he has had to wait in line for days just to refill.

Other drivers say the increase still does not fully match their real operating costs, while passengers complain that even small fare changes are becoming difficult to absorb as the price of food and other essentials continues to rise.

Dawit, a teacher who is also pursuing a master's degree at Hawassa University, said the tariff changes feel disconnected from what ordinary people can actually afford. Shopkeeper Ali voiced a similar concern, saying higher transport costs eventually feed into the prices of food, clothing, and other goods.

Public transportation specialist Ato Degeife said the tariff revision was mandated at the federal level and was based on research that considered fuel prices, spare-part costs, vehicle type, and road conditions. He said the city is also responsible for enforcing the legal rate and penalizing drivers who demand more than the official tariff.

Officials say the bureau is trying to communicate the new rates through social media and local broadcasting, but public frustration remains high as both operators and passengers struggle to cope with the wider rise in living costs.

Tourism Lakeside Project

Hawassa City Plans Lakeside Development Project to Improve Tourism and Public Spaces

City officials say the new lakeside project is designed to protect the environment while creating more welcoming spaces for residents and visitors.

The Hawassa City Administration has launched a new development project around Lake Hawassa aimed at improving tourism, protecting the environment, and creating better public spaces for residents.

According to Hawassa City Construction Department Design and Contract Administration Team Leader Mr. Elias Welde, the work forms part of the city's broader urban development plan and will focus on improving the lakeside area while maintaining environmental sustainability.

Officials say the project will help protect the lake environment while also creating modern recreational spaces for both residents and visitors.

Plans include recreational areas, walking paths, and other facilities meant to make the lakeside more attractive and accessible to the public. City leaders also expect the project to support the local economy by drawing more tourists to the area.

Residents have welcomed the initiative. One resident, Mr. Yayenehtesfaye Mengistu, said the development could make the lakeside more attractive and provide better places for families and visitors to spend time.

He also urged the city to keep recreational services affordable so that the wider community can benefit, not only tourists or higher-income residents.

Officials say the development will be implemented in phases and is expected to contribute to the long-term growth of Hawassa City.

Sports Youth Development

From Dust to Glory: Hawassa's Sports Infrastructure Inspires a New Generation

Around Korem Mada, young athletes keep training through dust and limited space, hoping local investment can match their ambition.

As the sun sets over Korem Mada, clouds of dust rise from the historic field where hundreds of young athletes gather daily, chasing dreams of becoming professional footballers.

The ground is known for producing notable Ethiopian players such as Adane Girma and Shimelis Bekele, and it remains a symbol of hope for many of Hawassa's children.

For families across the city, football is more than a pastime. It is a pathway to represent the country, build a better future, and take part in a wider culture of health and community development.

Hawassa City Administration Sports Commission officials say the city has between 15 and 20 sports fields. Many fall below standard, but they are still heavily used for exercise and training.

Commissioner Damarso Dona said new facilities and cycling paths introduced through urban development projects have encouraged more people to take part in physical activity. The city has also launched mass sports programs such as street races and organized cycling events.

Challenges remain. Coach Sintayehu Meshesha, who runs a youth football project, said limited space forces his team to train on only half of the Korem Mada field three times a week.

Parents share similar concerns and want stronger support for youth development in neighborhoods across the city. Officials acknowledge the poor condition of many fields but say new standards and a sports policy are expected to improve the sector in the coming years.

Public Safety Crime Watch

Mobile Phone Theft on the Rise Across Hawassa as Thieves Shift Tactics

Police and residents say phone theft is becoming more organized, with cases reported in dormitories, on the street, and from passing motorbikes.

Phone theft is becoming a growing concern in Hawassa City as thieves use different methods such as pickpocketing, breaking into university dormitories, and snatching phones from pedestrians using motorbikes.

According to Hawassa City Police Criminal Investigation Department Vice Inspector Yrdachew Angiso, the city records two to three phone theft cases every week.

He said many suspects are young people from rural areas or the streets who are influenced by peer pressure or drawn to crime by the hope of quick money. Some operate in groups and frequently change tactics, making them harder to catch.

Victims say many thefts happen in the evening or late at night when people are less alert. Phones are often taken while owners are sleeping, walking alone, or using public transportation.

University student Kena Deresa said her phone was stolen while she was asleep in her dormitory. Another student, Etsubdink Abraham, said men on a motorbike snatched her friend's phone while the student was returning to campus from work.

Hawassa resident Ethiopia Kebede said her phone disappeared while she was visiting a friend near Piassa, showing that theft is also common in busy public areas.

Police say they are working through the Community Policing Program and using both uniformed and plainclothes officers across the city. Residents are being advised to stay alert, especially in crowded or isolated areas, and to avoid openly displaying phones in public.
