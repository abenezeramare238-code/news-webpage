By selamawit dita

Rising Goods Prices Keep Squeezing Families in Hawassa

Residents say daily essentials remain painfully expensive even as national inflation cools.

Hawassa, Ethiopia , For Genet, a mother of living in the city of Hawassa, the daily trip to the market has turned into a exercise in painful choices. A 5-liter bottle of cooking oil once priced between 1,300 and 1,600 Birr now costs as much as 2,500 Birr. “We’ve cut back as much as we can, rationing every drop,” she says. “I don’t know how much longer we can manage.”

Traders point to fuel and supply chain shocks

Local traders say they have little choice but to raise prices. “Transport costs have exploded,” one shopkeeper explains. “When fuel prices go up, everything we sell follows.” Disruptions in national supply chains worsened by ongoing foreign currency shortages mean restocking shelves is slower and more expensive.

Economist: Global and domestic crises collide

Mr. Henok Sisay, an economist and lecturer at Hawassa University, says two overlapping shocks are to blame.

🌍 Globally, the Russia-Ukraine war has destabilized supply chains and driven up import costs for fuel and commodities. Domestically, the recent conflict in northern Ethiopia reduced local production, drained foreign currency

Reserves, and limited the flow of goods to markets like Hawassa.

Glimmer of hope? Inflation slows but pain remains Mr. Henok points to a surprising development, national inflation has eased sharply from 36% to around 9%. Tight monetary policies, he notes, are beginning to stabilize the economy. Yet for residents like Genet, that statistic feels abstract. “They say inflation is slowing, but I still pay double what I paid last year,” she says.

Key takeaway

Falling national inflation does not immediately translate to lower shelf prices. For now, families continue to ration, and traders continue to struggle with no quick fix in sight.

By mahlet teshome and yabsera samual

Traditional and Modern Therapy Blend Near Hawassa

Practitioners near Wendo Genet say sand-walking therapy is gaining attention as communities revisit affordable, culturally-rooted care.

Hawassa, April 2026 — Traditional medicine is gaining renewed attention in Hawassa, with increasing interest in reflexology-like treatments alongside modern healthcare approaches.

A local doctor reports that a unique therapy session is currently taking place in Wendo Genet, where patients walk barefoot on warm sand as part of treatment.

“Walking on sand helps stimulate foot pressure points, improve circulation, and reduce stress,” doctor yodahe explained.

“We are now studying these methods to align them with modern medical understanding.” The practice reflects a wider movement in Ethiopia to combine traditional healing with modern medicine, including research, standardization, and clinical evaluation.

Health experts say these therapies are affordable and culturally accepted, but emphasize the need for scientific validation to safely integrate them into formal healthcare systems.

Transport Tariff Watch

By meti gemechu

Hawassa Faces a Rising Tariff Burden as Commuters and Drivers Push Back

New transport rates, fuel shortages, and higher spare-part costs are intensifying pressure on both operators and passengers.

HAWASSA, Sidama Region A wave of frustration is sweeping through the city of Hawassa as drivers, students, and merchants grapple with a significant rise in transportation costs. The recent tariff increases, coupled with “chronic fuel shortages”and the rising price of spare parts, have left many residents questioning the city’s economic stability and the government’s ability to protect the community’s purchasing power. Drivers and Residents at a Breaking Point For many, the new rates have made daily life and business operations nearly untenable. “Arega”, a driver operating routes from Hawassa to major hubs like Addis Ababa and Adama, reports that a “10 birr increase”has been added to all routes due to fuel costs. He noted that fuel shortages are so severe he recently had to wait in line for five days to refill his tank.

The sentiment is shared by those commuting shorter distances. “Mebrate”, who drives from Hawassa to Yirgalem, argued that even with the hike, the tariff does not reflect the reality of fuel prices doubling. Conversely, commuters like “Dawit”, a teacher and graduate student, argue that the increases ignore the financial reality of the average citizen. According to Dawit, the price of basic necessities like food and beverages has already surged by 50%, leading many residents to “walk short distances” rather than pay for transport.

Local shopkeeper “Ali” emphasized that these costs create a ripple effect throughout the economy. “For us merchants, it forces us into a difficult position where we have no choice but to raise the prices of food, clothing, and other essentials,” he explained, calling on the government to better balance revenue needs with the actual cost of living.

Government Cites Federal Mandate and Rising Costs Addressing the public’s frustration, “Ato Degeife”, a public transportation specialist for the Hawassa region, clarified that the tariff hike was not a local decision but a “federal mandate”signed by the transport minister. Degeife explained that the new rates are based on detailed research that accounts for:
  • Vehicle type and age.
  • Road conditions.
  • Surging overheads: Petrol prices jumped from 91 birr per liter last year to 129 birr today, alongside significant increases in spare part costs.

Specific increases include a “2.95 birr per kilometer hike”for mid-sized “Dolphin” vehicles and “2.42 birr per kilometer” for larger public buses.

Enforcement and Public Information To prevent price gouging, authorities are conducting inspections “three times a month” to ensure drivers are not charging more than the statutory rates. Degeife warned that those found violating the law will face punishment.

The bureau is attempting to keep the public informed through its “Facebook page”, which has 10,000 followers, its Telegram channel, and airtime on “Sidama Broadcasting Corporation (SBC) radio”. Despite these efforts, some local bureaus, including the Hawassa City Public Transport Bureau, could not be reached for further comment on the ongoing crisis.

By dawit tolossa

From Dust to Dreams: Hawassa's Young Footballers Rise at Korem Meda

The historic field keeps producing ambition even as coaches and families call for better sports facilities across the city.

As the sun dips below the horizon, clouds of dust rise over Korem Meda, where hundreds of young athletes gather each day hoping to build football careers from a shared community ground.

The field is known for producing Ethiopian players such as Adane Girma and Shimelis Bekele, and it still stands as a symbol of hope for many children in Hawassa.

City officials say that enthusiasm for sport is doing more than filling fields. They see it as part of a wider push toward health, community development, and stronger neighborhood life.

According to the Hawassa City Administration Sports Commission, the city has between 15 and 20 sports fields. Many fall below standard, but they remain heavily used for training and exercise.

Commissioner Damarso Dona said recent urban projects, including cycling paths and recreational spaces, have increased participation. The city has also introduced street races and organized cycling events.

Coaches say space is still a major challenge. Sintayehu Meshesha, who runs a youth football project, said his team trains on only half of the Korem Meda field three times a week.

Authorities say new standards introduced under a sports policy should help improve facilities in the coming years, but for now young players are still chasing big dreams from dusty ground.

Community Humanitarian Work

By tgist anteneh

Rohobot Founder Addise Mekebo Turned a Dream Into a Lifeline for Vulnerable Children

After leaving a 23-year career in education, Addise Mekebo says faith, sacrifice, and community support helped build a home for abandoned infants.

Addise Mekebo's story began in Kembata, where he worked as a teacher and school principal for 23 years before a dream pushed him toward a very different life.

In that dream, he saw distressed children wandering the streets and heard a message telling him to care for them like a father. He later left his stable job and founded Rohobot, a humanitarian organization whose name reflects the idea that God makes room for people to flourish.

The early years were extremely difficult. Mekebo says he struggled to secure legal permits, lost the security of a monthly salary, and at one point had so little money that he went through the Easter holiday without food until a friend helped him.

The COVID-19 period brought the deepest crisis. Donations stopped, board members left, and he was left caring for 25 infants with no clear way to feed them.

Mekebo says he responded by returning to Durame and selling inherited land to buy milk and diapers. After that sacrifice, support started to grow, including a 17-month rent waiver from his landlady and wider public attention.

Today Rohobot works with police in Sidama and nearby areas to rescue abandoned infants. The organization says 310 children have since been legally adopted into new families.

Mekebo's message remains simple: people should stop living only for themselves and share even a small part of the world's problems.

By Nejat muhammed

Mobile Phone ThefT on The Rise in hawassa as Thieves Change TaCTiCs

Mobile phone theft is becoming an increasing concern in Hawassa City, as criminals adopt new and more sophisticated methods to carry out their activities. Incidents range from pickpocketing and dormitory break-ins to phone snatching by thieves using motorbikes.

According to Vice Inspector Yrdachew Angiso from the Hawassa City Police Criminal Investigation Department, the city records an average of two to three phone theft cases each week. He noted that many suspects are young individuals, often from rural areas or living on the streets, who are influenced by peer pressure or driven by the desire to earn quick money. “Some offenders operate in organized groups and frequently change their tactics, which makes them difficult to identify and arrest,” he explained. In recent years, the number of mobile phone users in Hawassa has increased significantly, especially among students and young people. As smartphones become more valuable and widely used for communication, education, and financial services, they have also become attractive targets for theft. Many of these crimes take place during the evening or late at night when people are less alert. Victims report that phones are commonly stolen while they are asleep, walking alone, or using public transportation. In some cases, thieves carefully observe their targets before taking action. Kena Deresa, a university student, shared her experience of losing her phone while sleeping in her dormitory. “Someone quietly entered our room at night and took my phone while it was charging. I didn’t wake up until my roommate shouted,” she said. Although the incident was reported to the police, her phone has not yet been recovered. Another student, Etsubdink Abraham, described a case in which her friend’s phone was snatched by two men on a motorbike while returning to campus. “Since then, I avoid walking while holding my phone in my hand,” she said, explaining how the incident changed their daily behavior. Phone theft is also common in crowded public areas and on public transportation. Ethiopia Kebede, a Hawassa resident, said her phone went missing while she was visiting a friend near Piassa. “I was in a busy place, but somehow my phone disappeared. I only realized it a few minutes later,” she explained. The increasing number of theft cases is not only causing financial loss but also creating fear among residents. Many people now feel unsafe using their phones in public places or even inside their dormitories. Students, in particular, are becoming more cautious in their daily movements. In response to the rising incidents, the police are working closely with the community through the Community Policing Program to prevent such crimes. Both uniformed and plainclothes officers have been deployed in different parts of the city to improve security and respond quickly to reported cases. Police advise residents to remain vigilant, especially in crowded or isolated areas, and to avoid displaying their phones openly in public. They also recommend keeping doors locked at night, securing personal belongings, and reporting suspicious activities immediately.As mobile phone theft continues to rise, both law enforcement and the community must work together to improve safety. Raising public awareness and taking preventive measures are essential steps toward reducing such crimes and ensuring a safer environment for everyone in Hawassa.

Media Experts Warn of Escalating Risks in the Digital Age

Hawassa, Ethiopia — Have you ever shared a breaking news story on social media, only to find out minutes later it was unverified or false? You aren’t alone. As digital feeds move faster than newsrooms can type, the line between "first" and "factual" is becoming dangerously blurred.

In-depth Analysis: Media outlets must utilize experts to explain the "why" behind the news. Fact-Checking: Journalists must prioritize rigorous verification to combat the "fake news" currently surfacing online. Expertise: Because news circulates on digital platforms first, the value of traditional media now lies in its depth. "The emergence of digital media has weakened the core principles of journalism ethics," says Damte. By By Dereje Fikre Debele

Hawassa Residents Raise Health Concerns Over Nearby solide Waste Dump.

Residents in the Diaspora area of Hawassa City have raised serious health and social concerns over a nearby waste dumping site that has been expanding for more than a decade.

The residents say the site, located less than 20 meters from their homes, produces a strong odor and poses risks to their health, prompting calls for urgent government action. Meseret's Bushune home Mrs. Meseret Bushune, a local resident, said she has lived in the area for six years but is now struggling due to the unpleasant smell from the waste. “Because of the terrible odor, I cannot ensure the health of my children or live in peace,” she said. Similarly, Mr. Matiyas Gabiso explained that he has lived in the area for eight years and has witnessed the situation worsen over time. “For over ten years, waste transported from the city has been dumped very close to our homes. We cannot live comfortably,” he said.

Calls for Government Intervention

He added that since 2013, residents have repeatedly appealed to the city administration to relocate the dumping site or provide a sustainable solution.

City Officials Acknowledge the Problem

In response to the complaints, Hawassa City officials acknowledged the seriousness of the problem.

Mr. Elias Zeleke, Director of the Sanitation and Green Development Directorate at the Hawassa City Administration, confirmed that the residents’ concerns are valid.

Intended solution for the problem

“To solve this problem at its root, we are working to relocate the dumping site to a more suitable area within a year. A new site has already been selected,” he said.

He also noted that the landfill near the Diaspora area presents a complex environmental and public health challenge.

Unfinished Waste Management Project

According to residents, a project aimed at improving the dumping site was launched in 2014 with financial support of more than 45 million birr from the Japanese government. However, the project was interrupted for unknown reasons and has not been completed. Mr. Elias confirmed that the project was left unfinished due to administrative and technical challenges. Additionally, officials reported that for the past ten years, more than 27,000 tons of waste from city districts and surrounding areas have been dumped at the site annually.

New Plans to Improve Waste Management

Mr. Mengesha Dedebo an expert from the urban Sanitation and Green Development office tated that the city administration is working on new initiatives to improve waste management and reduce risks to residents.

Recycling Efforts and Limitations4 He explained that efforts are being made to convert waste into useful resources rather than simply disposing of it. “We have started recycling up to 30 percent of the accumulated waste to produce natural fertilizer through composting,” he said. He also noted that several organizations are participating in recycling efforts. However, the volume of waste currently exceeds the available technological capacity.
